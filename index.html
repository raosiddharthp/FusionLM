Abstract

Enterprise finance teams routinely deploy two independent fine-tuned language models: one specialised on quantitative reasoning — earnings analysis, covenant interpretation, DCF modelling — and one on client-facing communication. Running both in a pipeline doubles inference cost, introduces latency, and requires a hand-off layer that degrades output coherence. This document describes FusionLM, an open-source system that merges two specialist models at the weight level using SLERP, TIES-Merging, and DARE, producing a single model that preserves both domain capabilities without retraining.

The primary audience is ML engineering teams at asset managers, investment banks, and fintech platforms who have already fine-tuned specialist models and need a cost-effective path to combining them. FusionLM runs entirely on CPU — no GPU required for the merge step — and the resulting model is evaluated against both source specialists on domain-specific benchmarks.

§1 Motivation

Enterprise AI teams at financial institutions have accumulated a quiet problem. Between 2023 and 2025, the standard practice was to fine-tune separate models for separate workflows: one model trained on 10-Ks, earnings transcripts, and financial covenants; another trained on client communication, meeting summaries, and CRM tone. Both models are good at their jobs. Neither handles the other's domain.

Any real analyst workflow — drafting a client memo that explains a DCF analysis, or writing a risk summary that is both numerically precise and legible to a non-technical LP — requires both capabilities simultaneously. Today, teams solve this in one of three ways, all of them expensive.

Design constraint
FusionLM requires no new training data, no GPU for the merge step, and no changes to downstream serving infrastructure. The integration surface is a single merged model file — a drop-in replacement for whichever specialist was being served.

§2 Problem Statement

The core problem is a mismatch between how models are trained and how analysts actually work. Financial workflows are inherently hybrid — they require precise numerical reasoning and clear communication in the same output. Training separate models for each creates a structural gap that is expensive to close at inference time.

§3 Merge Techniques

FusionLM implements three merge algorithms, each operating on the delta between fine-tuned weights and the shared base model. All three require no gradient computation — the merge step runs on CPU in under 20 minutes for 7B models.

Figure 3                                         
Weight-space geometry — where each technique operates
3.1 · SLERP
Spherical Linear Interpolation
Interpolates between two model weight tensors along a geodesic arc on a hypersphere, preserving the geometric properties of weight space. Unlike linear interpolation, SLERP maintains constant angular velocity during blending.
θ_t = sin((1-t)Ω)/sin(Ω)·θ_A + sin(tΩ)/sin(Ω)·θ_B
Best for: two models with similar architecture, where a smooth interpolation across the weight manifold is desired. Parameter t ∈ [0,1] controls blend ratio.
3.2 · TIES-Merging
Trim, Elect Sign, Merge
Addresses two failure modes of naive weight averaging: redundant delta parameters that dilute signal, and sign conflicts where models disagree on the direction of a weight update.
τ̃ = γ · sign(τ̂) ⊙ |τ̂| where τ̂ resolves by sign election
Best for: merging 3+ models. Automatically handles interference between task vectors. More robust than SLERP when domain gap is large.
3.3 · DARE
Drop And REscale
Randomly zeros out a fraction p of each fine-tuned model's delta parameters, then rescales the survivors by 1/(1−p) to preserve the expected embedding magnitude. Works because delta parameters are surprisingly sparse.
δ̃ᵢ = δᵢ/(1-p) · Bernoulli(1-p)
Best for: augmenting TIES (DARE-TIES). Effective with drop rates up to 90%. Reduces interference between specialist models before the TIES election step.

§6 Evaluation

FusionLM is evaluated against both source specialists on three benchmark axes: quantitative reasoning (FinBench), general finance knowledge (MMLU Finance), and factual reliability (TruthfulQA). A two-model pipeline baseline (Model A → Model B hand-off) is included as the incumbent cost comparator.

Key result
DARE-TIES achieves the highest overall score across all three benchmarks, outperforming the naive SLERP baseline on FinBench by 4.2 points and matching the two-model pipeline quality at 50% inference cost.

Limitations
SLERP is constrained to exactly two models — scaling to a third specialist (e.g. a regulatory model) requires switching to TIES or chaining merges. All three techniques require the source models to share an identical base architecture and tokenizer; merging BioMistral-7B with a Llama-3-8B would require architectural alignment first. Benchmark figures are indicative — exact results depend on the specific checkpoints used.
